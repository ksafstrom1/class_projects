Understanding Tornadoes

What Is a Tornado?

A tornado is a rapidly rotating column of air that extends from a thunderstorm down to the ground. These storms can cause major damage in just a few minutes. Tornadoes usually form when warm, moist air meets cool, dry air, creating strong winds and unstable weather conditions.

Where and When They Happen

Many tornadoes occur in an area of the United States known as Tornado Alley, including states like Texas, Oklahoma, and Kansas. However, tornadoes can form almost anywhere given the right weather setup. They are most common in spring and early summer when temperature contrasts are strongest.

Key Facts About Tornadoes

  • Wind speeds in strong tornadoes can exceed 200 miles per hour.
  • Some tornadoes reach over one mile wide.
  • Many tornadoes last only a few minutes but can cause extreme damage.
  • Doppler radar helps meteorologists detect rotation inside storms.
  • The Enhanced Fujita (EF) Scale rates tornado intensity from EF0 to EF5.
