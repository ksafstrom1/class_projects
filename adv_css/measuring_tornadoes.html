Enhanced Fujita (EF) Scale

The Enhanced Fujita Scale is used in the United States to rate the strength of tornadoes based on the damag they cause. It helps meteorologists and emergency managers describe how severe a tornado was in a clear andconsistent way.

How it all began: This system of rating was created by Ted Fujita. He studied at the University of Chicago. Ted Fujita first applied his tornado intensity scale during the 1974 Super Outbreak in the Midwest, though he had originally developed it in 1971. He studied the widespread damage from that historic outbreak to classify the strength of individual tornadoes and demonstrate the accuracy of his scale.

Fujita introduced what became known as the F-scale in a 1971 research paper. The system measures a tornado’s intensity by examining the destruction it causes, linking visible damage to estimated wind speeds.

The 1974 Super Outbreak provided the ideal opportunity to test the scale in real-world conditions. By mapping tornado paths and documenting their effects, Fujita was able to confirm that his approach could reliably compare the strength of different storms.

His method combined aerial photographs, ground-level damage surveys, and eyewitness reports. Using this information, he reconstructed how each tornado formed, traveled, and intensified, helping scientists better understand tornado behavior and refine future forecasting efforts.

Transition to the EF Scale: Over time, meteorologists recognized that while Fujita’s original scale was groundbreaking, it had limitations. The estimates of wind speed were based solely on visible damage, which could vary depending on building quality and materials. To improve accuracy, the National Weather Service and a team of engineers and atmospheric scientists developed the Enhanced Fujita, or EF Scale, which replaced the original F-scale in 2007. The EF Scale refined the relationship between wind speed and observed damage, providing more precise estimates while maintaining Fujita’s foundational idea of measuring tornado intensity through its impact on structures and vegetation.

Rating Estimated Wind Speed Typical Damage
EF0 65–85 mph Shallow-rooted trees pushed over, minor roof and siding damage.
EF1 86–110 mph Roofs stripped, mobile homes damaged or overturned, broken windows.
EF2 111–135 mph Roofs torn off well-built homes, large trees snapped or uprooted.
EF3 136–165 mph Entire stories of homes destroyed, severe damage to large buildings.
EF4 166–200 mph Well-constructed houses leveled, cars thrown, strong structures heavily damaged.
EF5 Over 200 mph Strong frame homes swept away, steel-reinforced buildings damaged, incredible destruction.
